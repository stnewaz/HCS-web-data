4.
If a student expresses suicidal thoughts, you should:

1. Ensure their immediate safety by getting them help right away (e.g., call 911) and do not leave them alone.
2. If they are confident in their short-term safety, provide a non-urgent referral to a mental health professional and acknowledge that it is their choice to take the referral.
3. Clarify what help looks like and reassure them that services are confidential.
4. For emergency situations, contact the Office of Student Support, Rights, and Responsibilities (SSRR) to provide an update, and a Case Manager will follow up with the student.

6.
A non-urgent referral for a student in distress is when a person is confident in their short-term safety. The goal is to help get them connected to a mental health professional, acknowledging that it is the student’s choice to take the referral and reinforcing that taking that step may help them reach positive change.

8.
Some protective factors against suicide include:

- Strong personal relationships
- Access to mental health care
- Cultural or religious beliefs that discourage suicide
- A sense of responsibility to family or others
- Effective coping and problem-solving skills
- Positive self-esteem
- Feeling connected to community or social supports

9.
- Prioritize your own well-being on an ongoing basis.
- Recognize that supporting students can be heavy and requires self-care.
- Set boundaries to maintain your own mental health.
- Utilize available resources for self-care.
- Ensure you are well, as this enables you to better support others.

To follow up with a student after referring them to a resource, you should: 

1. Check back with the individual after some time has passed to ask how they are doing and if they have had a chance to connect with a formal resource.

3. Ask if there is anything you can do to make the connection easier.

14.
Emotional reactivity or irritability in students refers to noticeable changes in their emotional responses, where they may become more easily upset, frustrated, or angry than usual. This can manifest as heightened sensitivity to stressors, increased agitation, or quick shifts in mood. It is often a sign that a student may be experiencing distress or struggling with underlying issues.

2. Check-in with the student by asking open-ended questions like, "I’ve noticed you haven’t been attending class regularly, are you okay?"
3. Listen in a caring and non-judgmental way to create a safe space for them to share.
4. Offer support by suggesting they speak to a professional, such as Health & Counselling, and offer to help set up an appointment.
5. Follow-up with the student after some time to see how they are doing and if they have connected with any resources. Encourage them to reach out again if needed.
