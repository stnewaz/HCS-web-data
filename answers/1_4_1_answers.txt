1.
The main goal of Health Promotion at SFU is to create healthy environments, improve health equity, and support student mental health and well-being.

2.
Planning for SFU’s Healthy Campus Community Initiative began in 2010.

3.
The Healthy Campus Community Initiative follows the World Health Organization (WHO) Healthy University Framework.

4.
SFU supports graduate students by providing resources and initiatives that promote well-being, helping them manage academic, research, and life commitments. The university acknowledges the unique challenges faced by Teaching Assistants (TAs) and Tutor Markers (TMs) who balance their roles as both students and teachers. Additionally, faculty and staff create resources and activities to foster well-being in learning environments, which aids graduate students in their dual responsibilities.

5.
Resources being developed to support well-being in learning environments include in-class activities, teaching practices, content to share with students, and a presentation slide deck for sharing health and well-being information.

6.
Well-designed physical spaces boost mood, social connectedness, and learning, thereby supporting student well-being.

7.
Academic departments and faculties significantly shape students' university experiences and play a vital role in promoting student well-being and fostering a sense of community.

8.
The purpose of the SFU Supporting Students in Distress Response Guide is to provide resources and tips for faculty, instructors, staff, and Teaching Assistants/Tutor Markers on how to support students in difficulty and take care of themselves.

9.
Faculty can measure the impacts of their efforts in promoting well-being by using questions designed for this purpose on Student Evaluation of Teaching and Courses (SETC) or within any other feedback and course evaluation tools they utilize.

10.
SFU Health Promotion provides a presentation slide deck that can be used to share health and well-being information and supports with students at the beginning or end of classes. Additionally, they offer a template to explore how to share links to resources with students within feedback on their work.

11.
Instructors can support inclusivity in the classroom by:

1. Supporting the declaration of self-identified pronouns during introductions.
2. Introducing tools and topics related to well-being in class.
3. Creating resources to foster well-being and inclusivity for diverse students.
4. Facilitating quick class check-in activities to promote social connection.
5. Developing classroom activities and teaching practices that enhance student support.

12.
Remote learning environments come with unique challenges that can impact student well-being, including difficulties in social connection, increased feelings of isolation, and the need for resilience while navigating these situations.

13.
Student health is a key predictor of success and retention in post-secondary education because it significantly influences learning, academic performance, and overall well-being. Healthy students are more likely to engage positively in their studies, participate in campus activities, and develop supportive relationships, all of which contribute to their academic success and likelihood to remain in school.

14.
A draft/work-in-progress list of resources has been compiled to support SFU FHS faculty in creating a safer and more inclusive university environment for trans, gender non-binary, and other gender diverse students. Additionally, faculty members are encouraged to declare self-identified pronouns and introduce tools related to well-being in their classes.

15.
Instructors can promote social connection among students by facilitating quick class check-in activities at the beginning of the hour and supporting the declaration of self-identified pronouns in a “pronoun round” during introductions. Additionally, spending time in class to introduce tools and topics related to well-being can also foster connections.
