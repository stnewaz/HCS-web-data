1.
UVic is committed to providing a safe and inclusive living, working, and learning environment for people of all genders, as part of their broader commitment to creating an inclusive space where everyone feels a sense of belonging. This commitment is expressed through strategies and priorities in UVic’s Strategic Plan, Equity Action Plan, and the suite of plans and policies that describe expectations for behavior and services on campus.

4.
When planning for the first class, consider options for taking attendance since the official class list may not reflect chosen names for trans, Two-Spirit, and non-binary students. Some options include:

1. Allow students to indicate a “preferred name” on their Netlink ID profile, but be aware that not all students may have provided this information yet.
2. If you need to take attendance at the first class, use alternative methods that do not rely solely on official names.
3. Once you are aware of a preferred name, ensure that you use it whenever you need to refer to a student by name, in writing or verbally.

7.
1. Be aware of the diversity of gender identities and avoid reinforcing binary notions of gender.
2. Provide a safe space and ensure that students know the purpose of discussions in advance.
3. Frame discussions appropriately and provide concrete data points for context.
4. Do not single out trans, Two-Spirit, or non-binary individuals during conversations.
5. Prepare to manage potential conflicts and know available support resources.
6. Approach discussions with care, acknowledging the potential for trauma.
7. Set parameters for civil and respectful discourse.
8. Inform students about the discussion topic beforehand.
9. Recognize the contextual implications of gender identity discussions, especially related to historical factors.

8.
Faculty should prepare to manage conflict around topics of gender identity by:

1. Knowing their responsibilities to support trans, Two-Spirit, and students who may become distressed by these conversations.
2. Being aware of available resources for support.
3. Holding conversations in context, acknowledging the impact of colonization and European influence on homophobic or transphobic values within Indigenous communities.
4. Setting clear parameters for civil and respectful discourse during discussions.
5. Being prepared for the possibility that discussions may be traumatic for trans, Two-Spirit, and non-binary individuals.

9.
Students can approach the professor, lab instructor, event organizer, chair of the department, co-op advisor, Equity and Human Rights (EQHR), Office of the Ombudsperson, or UVSS Advocacy groups with concerns about their experiences in classes or activities.

10.
Students facing challenges in co-op placements can contact their co-op advisor for support.

11.
Students can access support from Equity and Human Rights (EQHR) by:

1. Talking about their concerns with the professor, lab instructor, event organizer, or whoever is in charge of organizing the activity.
2. Contacting the chair of the department to discuss any academic course concerns.
3. Consulting with their co-op advisor for challenges in a co-op placement.
4. Having a confidential consultation with EQHR advisors for any situation.
5. Bringing a support person to any conversations with EQHR.
6. Reaching out to the Office of the Ombudsperson for independent, impartial, and confidential support.

12.
The Office of the Ombudsperson at UVic serves as an independent, impartial, and confidential resource for undergraduate and graduate students and other members of the University of Victoria community. It helps resolve student problems or disputes fairly for issues that fall within their jurisdiction.

13.
The Anti-Violence Project provides support, education, and resources to all students, staff, faculty, and other community members impacted by gender-based violence. Their services can be accessed during drop-in hours or by appointment, and are provided by staff and volunteers with training and lived experience supporting and being trans, non-binary, and Two-Spirit folks.

14.
Faculty can prepare for potential distress among students during discussions on gender identity by:

1. Knowing their responsibilities to support trans, Two-Spirit, and non-binary students who may become distressed.
2. Being aware of available resources for support.
3. Holding conversations in context, acknowledging the impact of historical factors such as colonization on attitudes towards gender identity.
4. Setting clear goals for the discussion linked to learning outcomes.
5. Informing students in advance about the discussion and its purpose.
6. Providing concrete data points for discussion and framing the issue appropriately.
7. Avoiding singling out any trans, Two-Spirit, or non-binary individuals in the classroom.
8. Establishing parameters for civil, respectful discourse.
9. Describing and offering supports in multiple venues, formats, and times for students to access.

15.
Students should talk about their concerns with the professor, lab instructor, event organizer, or whoever is in charge of organizing the activity. For a question or concern with an academic course, they can contact the chair of the department. For a challenge in a co-op placement, they can contact their co-op advisor. If things get complicated, they can have a confidential consultation with someone at Equity and Human Rights (EQHR). They can also consult with the Office of the Ombudsperson for independent and impartial support.
