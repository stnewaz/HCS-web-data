1.
SFU’s Reconciliation Reports highlight the story of reconciliation at SFU, the current status, and future goals in the journey together. They include Aboriginal Strategic Initiatives (ASI)-funded work, progress on the 34 calls to action from the SFU Aboriginal Reconciliation Council’s (SFU-ARC) 2017 report, and amplify Indigenous voices and knowledge.

2.
The 34 calls to action in the SFU Aboriginal Reconciliation Council’s 2017 report are significant as they frame and respond to key issues, challenges, and opportunities related to reconciliation at SFU. They guide the university's collective efforts to address the Truth and Reconciliation Commission’s 2015 report and serve as a roadmap for collaborative initiatives aimed at improving Indigenous students' access, transition, and retention, as well as enhancing engagement with Indigenous governments, communities, and organizations.

3.
SFU incorporates Indigenous languages throughout its Reconciliation Reports to amplify the voices of Indigenous peoples and honor Indigenous knowledge.

5.
The purpose of the Aboriginal Strategic Initiative (ASI) Final Report is to summarize the work undertaken at SFU related to reconciliation, highlight progress on the 34 calls to action from the SFU Aboriginal Reconciliation Council’s 2017 report, and showcase various projects and initiatives aimed at improving Indigenous student access, transition, and retention, as well as enhancing engagement with Indigenous governments, communities, and organizations.

6.
The SFU-ARC’s 2017 report, "Walk This Path With Us," helps to frame and respond to key issues, challenges, and opportunities that SFU faces in its efforts to respond to the Truth and Reconciliation Commission’s 2015 report and Calls to Action.

7.
The Looking Forward ... Indigenous Pathways To and Through Simon Fraser University report addresses Call to Action 15 from the SFU-ARC's Walk This Path With Us report, recommending ways to improve Indigenous students’ access, transition, and retention.

8.
The Engagement with Indigenous Governments, Communities and Organizations report provides insights from the first survey of its kind for faculty and staff on SFU’s engagement with Indigenous governments, communities, and organizations. It aims to deepen understanding of current external Indigenous engagement activities and showcases more than 50 examples of engagement initiatives with 55 Indigenous governments and communities, as well as 16 other Indigenous organizations. These engagements occur not only in British Columbia but also across Canada, in 11 out of 13 provinces and territories, and internationally in Guatemala and New Zealand.
