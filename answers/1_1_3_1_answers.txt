Sexual health encompasses sexuality, sexual orientation, gender identity, gender expression, body image, sexual self-esteem, sexual and reproductive health care, reproduction, experiences of unwanted violence and coercion, consent and boundaries, relationships and intimacy, sexual and reproductive anatomy, pleasure, sexual activities, employment within the sexual health context, and the role of physical and mental health in overall sexual health.

2.
Sexuality includes how someone thinks about and expresses themselves as a sexual being, including thoughts, actions, fantasies, and sensuality.

3.
Sexual orientation refers to who someone is attracted to sexually, emotionally, and romantically, as well as how they identify and express that attraction. It is about feelings and identities, and can be fluid over time. Sexual behaviors, on the other hand, are the actual actions someone engages in sexually, which may not necessarily align with their sexual orientation.

4.
Gender identity can vary among individuals in that it encompasses a range of identities beyond the traditional binary of male and female. People may identify as a woman, man, both, in between, neither, as two-spirit, transgender, or anywhere along the gender spectrum. Additionally, individuals may express their gender identity in various ways, which can be consistent or change over time.

5.
Factors that influence body image in relation to sexual health include:

1. How someone sees and feels about their body.
2. Personal beliefs and feelings about one's physical appearance.
3. Societal standards of beauty and body norms.
4. Experiences of trauma or body shaming.
5. Cultural influences and messages regarding body image.
6. Mental health and self-esteem.
7. Physical health and changes in the body (e.g., aging, illness, surgery).
8. Media representation and portrayal of bodies.

6.
Sexual Self-Esteem is how someone sees and what they feel and believe about themselves as a sexual being.

7.
Sexual and Reproductive Health Care includes care, access, and experiences of medical health relating to sexual and reproductive areas such as: STIs and HIV, pap testing, prostate exams, pregnancy testing, birth control, abortion, birth, transgender care, etc.

9.
Experiences of unwanted violence and coercion, including sexual abuse and sexual assault, directly impact sexual health by creating fear, intimidation, and harm in personal sexual lives and relationships. Such experiences can affect an individual's ability to engage in sexual activities and maintain healthy relationships, as they may lead to issues with consent, boundaries, and overall sexual well-being. Recognizing the distinction between unwanted violence and consensual activities is crucial in understanding the complexities of sexual health.

10.
- Legal age of consent
- Understanding consent
- Personal boundaries
- Knowing how to give and receive consent
- Recognizing when consent is not given (verbally or non-verbally)
- Knowing how to respond when consent is not given

11.
Relationships and intimacy are critical components of sexual health as they encompass different types of relationships and dynamics, helping to identify when relationships are beneficial or harmful. Intimacy is an essential aspect of close relationships but does not always involve sexual activity. Healthy relationships can enhance sexual health by fostering emotional connections, trust, and communication, while unhealthy relationships can negatively impact an individual's sexual wellbeing.

12.
Sexual and reproductive anatomy includes how the sexual and reproductive parts of our bodies work, including any gender-based surgeries and being intersex.

13.
Pleasure is defined as our ability to receive and experience sexual pleasure whether in our bodies and/or minds.

14.
There are a wide range of sexual activities, and what is enjoyable and pleasurable varies from person to person.

15.
Employment within the sexual health context relates to sex work and the working conditions surrounding sex work.

16.
Physical and mental health can impact sexual health in several ways. For instance, physical changes due to puberty, aging, surgery, or illness can affect one's sexual experiences and overall well-being. Mental health, such as emotional states and mood, can influence sexual confidence and self-esteem. Access to healthcare, like birth control, can improve physical health, which may lead to a more positive emotional state and enhance sexual self-esteem. Overall, different types of health, including physical, mental, and sexual health, are interconnected and can significantly affect one another.

18.
Cultural background influences one’s perception of sexual health by shaping beliefs, values, and attitudes towards sexuality. It can dictate what is considered acceptable or taboo, affect communication about sexual health, and influence access to information and services. Cultural norms may also impact how individuals view concepts like consent, relationships, and gender identity, leading to different levels of comfort and openness in discussing sexual health issues. Additionally, societal messages from family, religion, and community can create feelings of shame or empowerment regarding sexual health.

20.
Social determinants of health can affect sexual health experiences in several ways, including:

1. **Income**: Limited financial resources may restrict access to sexual health services and education.
2. **Education**: Lack of education can lead to misinformation about sexual health, impacting decision-making and health outcomes.
3. **Employment**: Job stability and conditions can influence access to health care and create stress that affects sexual health.
4. **Childhood Development**: Early experiences and upbringing can shape attitudes and beliefs about sexual health.
5. **Food Security**: Access to nutritious food can influence overall health, including sexual health.
6. **Housing**: Stable housing is crucial for mental and physical well-being, which can affect sexual health.
7. **Health Services**: Availability and accessibility of sexual health services impact individuals' ability to seek care.
8. **Gender**: Gender identity and roles can influence access to sexual health resources and experiences of marginalization.
9. **Race and Ethnicity**: Discrimination and cultural barriers can affect access to care and support.
10. **Disability**: Individuals with disabilities may face additional challenges in accessing sexual health services.
11. **Indigenous Status**: Indigenous individuals may experience unique barriers related to cultural competency and access to health care.
12. **Social Marginalization**: Those who are marginalized (e.g., LGBTQ+ individuals) may encounter stigma and discrimination affecting their sexual health experiences.
13. **Social Services**: Availability of social support and services can impact overall health and well-being, including sexual health.
