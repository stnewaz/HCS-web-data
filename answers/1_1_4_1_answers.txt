To make an appointment with a primary care provider for hormone therapy, follow these steps:

1. Choose a primary care provider who is knowledgeable about gender-affirming care.
2. Contact their office directly to schedule an appointment.
3. If you feel anxious about discussing your needs, refer to resources like "Speaking with Your Provider about Gender-Affirming Care" for tips.
4. During your appointment, discuss your desire for hormone therapy and undergo a readiness assessment if required.
5. If your provider is not comfortable assessing readiness, they may refer you to another clinician or endocrinologist.

A hormone readiness assessment is an evaluation conducted by a qualified professional to determine if a patient is ready to begin hormone therapy. It is necessary because the WPATH Standards of Care require an assessment before hormone therapy starts to ensure that the patient meets the criteria for treatment and receives appropriate support.

9.
You do not necessarily need a mental health assessment to access hormone therapy if your primary care provider can provide the readiness assessment. However, if you choose to work with an endocrinologist, you will need to engage a mental health professional for a readiness assessment. Some primary care providers may recommend an assessment from a mental health provider, and while most mental health providers are private-pay, there are some public (free) options available that may require a referral.

10.
An endocrinologist's role in the hormone therapy process includes:

1. Requiring a hormone readiness assessment completed by another clinician before starting treatment.
2. Accepting hormone readiness assessment letters from specified providers.
3. Assessing the patient's readiness for hormone therapy based on established criteria.
4. Monitoring and managing the patient's hormone therapy once it begins.

11.
Yes, there are some public (free) options for hormone readiness assessments available in the community, though they may require a referral.

12.
During the hormone readiness assessment, you can expect the following:

1. A focus on supporting you, where the assessor or primary care provider will ask about your experiences and feelings related to hormone therapy.
2. Your prescribing provider may recommend a physical exam to check your heart and lungs.
3. You will likely undergo laboratory work, such as blood tests.
4. The assessment process will consider your unique needs, which may result in varying lengths of time for completion.
5. If you are a young person, additional factors related to your age and stage of development will be taken into account.
6. Mental health concerns or other issues will be considered but do not automatically disqualify you from eligibility for hormone therapy.

13.
The length of the assessment period depends on the health care provider, clinic protocols, and your needs. Assessments may take longer if someone has physical health, mental health, or substance use issues.

14.
When young people seek hormone therapy, their health care provider needs to consider additional factors related to their age and stage of development. Additionally, youth typically live with parents or guardians, who may need additional information or support related to gender-affirming health care.

15.
No, it is not necessary to be trans to be eligible for hormone therapy. Hormone therapy can be medically necessary for a range of people of all genders.

16.
The WPATH Standards of Care (SOC) are guidelines created by the World Professional Association for Transgender Health to help health care providers offer quality care for transgender and gender-diverse individuals. They outline best practices for diagnosis, treatment, and support, aiming to ensure respectful and affirming health care experiences. In relation to hormone therapy, the SOC specify criteria that must be met for eligibility before treatment begins. Assessors will confirm that individuals meet these criteria as part of the readiness assessment process.

Yes, in some cases, health care providers may prescribe hormones even when the WPATH criteria are not met. For example, a health care provider may use a harm reduction approach and prescribe hormone therapy as an alternative to someone obtaining hormones outside the health care system.

18.
No, having mental health concerns such as depression or anxiety does not necessarily disqualify you from accessing hormone therapy. These concerns will need to be reasonably managed prior to or during hormone therapy.

19.
Counseling is not typically required to get a prescription for hormones, although it can be very helpful for some people. It may be recommended or required if there are health risks associated with buying or borrowing hormone medications rather than getting a prescription from a health care provider.

20.
The risks of obtaining hormones outside the healthcare system include:

1. Uncertainty about the quality and dosage of the product, as they may be diluted or mixed with unknown substances.
2. Health risks associated with using hormone medications that are not prescribed by a healthcare provider.
3. Inability to determine the safe dosage required for optimal effects without proper blood work.
