1.
Stephen Brown's teaching philosophy supports student well-being by making him approachable and creating a supportive classroom environment. He believes that both the course content and the classroom environment can positively impact students' well-being.

2.
The classroom environment plays a crucial role in student well-being by creating a supportive and safe space that fosters respect, kindness, and diversity. A positive classroom culture encourages active involvement, promotes engagement as fellow scholars rather than passive recipients of knowledge, and allows flexibility in teaching methods. An organized and clear communication of expectations contributes to a sense of security, while enthusiasm and opportunities for application of material enhance student connection and motivation. Additionally, seeking feedback from students helps in understanding their needs and improving the learning experience.

3.
It is important to consider university students as adults because they should be treated as colleagues and partners rather than underlings, which fosters a more respectful and collaborative learning environment. Recognizing them as adults acknowledges their autonomy, promotes responsibility, and encourages active engagement in their education.

4.
Teaching assistants should be viewed as colleagues and partners, not underlings.

8.
1. Define the classroom as a safe place: orderly and businesslike, but informal. Encourage diversity, respect, and kindness.
2. Be flexible: have a plan, but adapt if relevant discussions arise or if explanations take longer than anticipated.
3. Communicate expectations clearly and provide a schedule of assignments and exams on the first day of class.
4. Engage students as fellow scholars rather than positioning oneself solely as the expert.
5. Create opportunities for active involvement with the material, such as activities that require students to write and reflect.
6. Seek feedback from students and summarize findings to share with the class.

9.
Flexibility is important in teaching because it allows instructors to adapt to interesting and relevant discussions that arise in class and to adjust to the pace of students' understanding, rather than rigidly adhering to a pre-set plan.

10.
Learning is more related to the amount of active involvement with the material than to the amount of material to which people are exposed.

11.
- Show enthusiasm for the course material.
- Provide anecdotes as examples.
- Use your whole body to communicate (e.g., demonstrate exercises).
- Engage students actively in discussions and activities.

12.
1. Providing students with notebooks to write about activities during class.
2. Engaging in solo and group activities related to course material.
3. Giving assignments that allow students to demonstrate their understanding, such as case study reports, video projects, or oral presentations.
4. Encouraging students to reflect on their learning through written feedback, like cue cards on class progress.

13.
1. Seek feedback from students by using anonymous cue cards to ask how the class is going, and summarize findings to share with the class.
2. Provide opportunities for students to apply material and receive feedback through activities, such as notebooks where comments can be made by the instructor and teaching assistant.
3. Incorporate feedback into letters of reference by highlighting specific projects or achievements of students rather than just their performance on quizzes and exams.
