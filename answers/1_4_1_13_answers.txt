1.
The role of the SFU Health Promotion team is to lead campus-wide efforts to improve student well-being, promote and protect student mental health, create healthy settings, enhance health equity, build community capacity for student mental health and well-being, and center student voice in the design of programs and initiatives. They also focus on decolonization, reconciliation, and advancing social justice principles.

2.
SFU Health Promotion contributes to student mental health and well-being by:

1. Leading campus-wide efforts to improve student well-being.
2. Developing and endorsing The Okanagan Charter, which is recognized internationally.
3. Promoting and protecting student mental health through the Healthy Campus Community Initiative.
4. Valuing and prioritizing health equity and mental health in all policies and practices.
5. Facilitating systemic actions to create healthy settings and enhance health equity.
6. Creating space for culturally inclusive practices and diverse perspectives on health.
7. Building partnerships to foster a healthy campus community.
8. Centering student voice in program design and decision-making.
9. Implementing comprehensive actions to embed well-being across the institution.
10. Using an asset-based approach to highlight and celebrate progress in well-being conditions.
11. Weaving principles of social justice and equity into all health promotion actions.
12. Committing to decolonization, reconciliation, and Indigenization in practices and programs.
13. Engaging in evidence-informed and reflexive practices for continual learning and growth.
14. Recognizing interconnectedness of ecological and social sustainability in health promotion activities.

3.
The Okanagan Charter is a framework for health-promoting universities and colleges that emphasizes the importance of health and well-being in higher education. It is significant because it has gained international recognition and endorsement from hundreds of institutions across 40 countries, highlighting its impact on promoting health equity and well-being in academic settings.

5.
SFU promotes health equity among students by:

1. Valuing, prioritizing, and advancing health equity, mental health, and student well-being through all policies, practices, spaces, and programming.
2. Leading and facilitating systemic action to create healthy settings and enhance health equity.
3. Integrating principles of social justice, accessibility, cultural safety, and equity into health promotion actions to reduce health disparities.
4. Committing to decolonization, reconciliation, and Indigenization in practices and programs.
5. Using an asset-based approach to highlight and celebrate shared progress in well-being.
6. Approaching work through anti-oppressive, anti-racist, trauma-informed, and healing-centered lenses.

6.
- Relationship building: Partnerships are central to health promotion and through collective, relational action, a healthy campus community can be created.
- Student voice: Students’ needs and goals are prioritized in decision making and program design.
- Systemic action: Comprehensive action is needed to embed well-being across individual and organizational levels and the academic mandate.
- Building on strengths: An asset-based approach seeks and expands exemplary actions for well-being while highlighting and celebrating progress.
- Equity and justice: Principles of social justice, accessibility, cultural safety, and equity are integrated into health promotion actions to reduce disparities.
- Decolonization, reconciliation, and Indigenization: Commitment to advancing these principles through practices and programs.
- Evidence-informed and reflexive practice: Commitment to continual learning and using diverse sources of knowledge to inform strategies.
- Ecological and social sustainability: Recognition of interconnectedness of health and well-being of communities, living beings, and the planet.

7.
SFU incorporates student voice in program design by holding students’ needs and goals at the heart of decision making and centering student voice in the design of programs and initiatives.

8.
Systemic action is important in promoting student well-being because it ensures that well-being is embedded across the institution at both individual and organizational levels, aligning with the academic mandate. It facilitates comprehensive approaches that address various factors affecting health and well-being, fostering a healthier campus community through collective and relational action.

9.
SFU addresses equity and justice in its health promotion efforts by incorporating principles of social justice, accessibility, cultural safety, and equity in all health promotion actions. This approach helps reduce health disparities and contributes to justice through anti-oppressive, anti-racist, trauma-informed, healing-centered, and socio-ecological lenses.

10.
SFU is committed to advancing reconciliation, decolonization, and Indigenization through its practices and programs. The university acknowledges its operation within a colonial institution that causes harm to Indigenous peoples and recognizes the responsibility to advance decolonization, equity, and justice.

11.
SFU ensures its practices are evidence-informed by committing to continual learning, reflexive practice, innovation, and growth. It draws upon diverse research, evidence, evaluation, lived experience, and various sources of knowledge to inform strategies.

12.
The One Health approach recognizes that the health and well-being of communities, living beings, and the planet are interconnected. At SFU, this approach is reflected in all health promotion activities, emphasizing the collective responsibility to the communities and the planet.

13.
A Healthy Campus Community at SFU is defined by the following aspects:

1. People, processes, and spaces contribute to well-being and success for all.
2. Valued, prioritized, and advanced health equity, mental health, and student well-being through policies, practices, and programming.
3. Systemic action is taken to create healthy settings and enhance health equity.
4. Holistic approaches that are culturally inclusive and consider diverse experiences and perspectives of health and well-being.
5. Strong partnerships and collective action that foster a healthy campus community.
6. Centering student voice and needs in decision-making and program design.
7. An asset-based approach that builds on strengths and celebrates shared progress.
8. Commitment to principles of social justice, accessibility, and equity to reduce health disparities.
9. Dedication to reconciliation, decolonization, and Indigenization in practices and programs.
10. Evidence-informed practices that involve continual learning and reflexive innovation.
11. Recognition of interconnectedness of health and sustainability for communities and the planet.
